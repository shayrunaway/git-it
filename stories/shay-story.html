All That Glitters Is Not Old

Glitter scientists, or glittists, have developed a new type of glitter that will no longer stick around for years to come--Going, Glowing, Gone. Gone are the days of leaving behind trails of sparkly conjunctivitis when you decide to break out your Wet 'n Wild eyeshadow palette gifted to you for Christmas '09. Going, Glowing, Gone is made up of naphthalene, the primary ingredient found in mothballs, a solid that slowly sublimates into the air. Due to the small surface area of a glit (A single piece of glitter), the glitter should disappear in 1-2 weeks when in a space with good air circulation. One reported downside is that dance clubs around the country have started smelling exactly like your favorite hide-and-seek spot at your grandmother's house. Additionally, elementary teachers whose classrooms participated in the test groups for Going, Glowing, Gone have noticed a statistically significant uptick in requests from students to play bingo and learn to crochet. Going, Glowing, Gone is patented under the sole proprietorship of Reinventing the Wheel, LLC. Reinventing the Wheel, LLC has made a pledge that for every 100 lbs of Going, Glowing, Gone sold they will ensure at least 10 lbs of microplastics are released into the waterways to offset the alarming reduction in pollution caused by biodegradable glitter.
